# Taiwan under gray-zone pressure

Taiwan in early 2026 faced mounting concern over gray-zone pressure from China rather than an immediate full-scale invasion scenario. Reported tactics included airspace pressure, maritime signaling, and suspected sabotage or interference affecting critical infrastructure such as undersea communications links.

Taiwan's answer remained the so-called porcupine strategy: building resilient, asymmetric defenses that are cheaper, more mobile, and harder to neutralize than a conventional prestige force. That logic favored systems such as drones, anti-ship missiles, and other mobile defensive tools.

Taiwan also continued to deepen defense procurement links with the United States. Approved purchases included systems such as Javelin anti-tank missiles and HIMARS, reinforcing the emphasis on denial rather than mirror-image escalation.

Politics added another layer. A notable visit by Kuomintang chair Cheng Li-wun to mainland China highlighted how Taiwan's domestic politics, cross-strait signaling, and future elections all intersect. That means a memory system should not reduce the Taiwan file to military facts alone. Political engagement and symbolic visits also matter.

The larger significance of this document is that it sits at the intersection of military strategy, domestic politics, and US-China competition.
