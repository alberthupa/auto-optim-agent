# US-China-EU trade and semiconductor tensions

The semiconductor sector remains one of the sharpest edges of competition between the United States and China. US export restrictions continue to limit China's access to advanced chips, but demand inside China remains strong. Chinese technology firms reportedly continued placing large orders for high-end processors for 2026 delivery, showing that export controls changed supply channels more than they eliminated demand.

This mismatch between demand and formal access created incentives for gray-market or covert routing. One reported case involved attempts to move large volumes of servers containing restricted Nvidia GPUs toward Chinese buyers. The broader lesson is that technology controls can redirect trade into opaque channels when demand remains strategically important.

The trade conflict is not limited to the United States and China. Europe is also dealing with Chinese industrial overcapacity in sectors such as electric vehicles, solar components, wind equipment, and semiconductors. European policy discussions increasingly frame this not only as a competition issue but as an economic security issue.

At the same time, Washington and Brussels are moving toward deeper coordination on critical minerals. The logic is straightforward: if rare earths and related inputs stay concentrated in Chinese-controlled supply chains, Western chip, defense, and clean-tech production remains exposed.

Diplomatically, officials held talks in Paris aimed at preparing a future Trump-Xi summit. Those talks suggested both sides still wanted a degree of managed stability, even while structural frictions remained unresolved. The same pattern applies across the file: tactical dialogue continues, but competition over chips, industrial policy, and supply chains remains fundamental rather than temporary.
