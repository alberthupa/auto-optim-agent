# NATO posture and allied burden sharing in 2026

NATO entered 2026 with a stronger defense-spending baseline than in previous years. According to alliance reporting, all allies had met the 2 percent of GDP defense spending target in 2025. European and Canadian members also significantly increased total defense outlays.

This shift matters because NATO is balancing multiple demands at once. The alliance still supports Ukraine against Russia, but it is also thinking about maritime security, long-range strike capacity, and faster adoption of emerging military technologies.

One pressure point came from instability around the Strait of Hormuz. US President Donald Trump sought more concrete allied commitments related to securing maritime flows there, revealing how Middle East crises can pull NATO attention away from Europe while still affecting European security and energy interests.

The burden-sharing story is therefore more complicated than a simple spending metric. More spending helps, but allies also face harder questions about where to deploy attention, assets, and political capital when several crises overlap.

For benchmarking purposes, this document is useful because it links three ideas that might otherwise be stored separately: the war in Ukraine, maritime insecurity around Hormuz, and the long-running alliance debate over who carries which part of the load.
