# Russia-Ukraine war, sanctions, and stalled diplomacy

By April 2026, diplomacy around the Russia-Ukraine war remained active but stuck. A temporary US sanctions waiver on Russian oil, introduced in mid-March during an energy shock linked to conflict involving Iran, was set to expire on April 11. The waiver was justified as a market-stabilization measure, but critics argued it risked giving Moscow revenue and reducing Western leverage.

Russian envoy Kirill Dmitriev visited Washington amid discussion of sanctions relief and possible peace terms. Yet negotiations remained hampered by deeper disagreements. Ukraine favored freezing the conflict roughly along current lines, while Russia rejected that approach and pushed for broader territorial concessions.

Reported Russian conditions for a larger settlement included a written pledge that NATO would stop expanding eastward and the easing of several sanctions. That made the talks difficult for both Ukraine and Western backers to accept.

An Orthodox Easter ceasefire was announced for a short period, but both sides quickly accused the other of violations. The ceasefire therefore illustrated the broader pattern: symbolic diplomatic gestures continued, but trust remained extremely low.

Meanwhile, the war's military and economic effects continued to evolve. Ukrainian attacks on Russian refining capacity reportedly damaged energy infrastructure enough to affect export decisions. NATO, for its part, kept emphasizing stronger conventional deterrence, faster technology adoption, and sustained allied defense spending.

The file should be read together with the broader risk and energy documents: the war in Ukraine was no longer only a European battlefield issue. It had become entangled with sanctions policy, oil-market management, and alliance politics.
