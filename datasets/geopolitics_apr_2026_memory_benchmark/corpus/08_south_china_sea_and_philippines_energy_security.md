# South China Sea tensions and the Philippines energy-security dilemma

The Philippines entered an energy emergency after major oil-supply disruptions linked to the wider Middle East crisis. Because it imports most of its oil from the Persian Gulf, the country faced immediate economic and transport risk when regional shipping and supply were disrupted.

That energy shock spilled into geopolitics. Manila explored whether it could obtain waivers that would allow purchases from sanctioned producers such as Iran or Venezuela. At the same time, the crisis increased the appeal of resetting parts of its relationship with Beijing, including discussion of possible joint oil exploration in the South China Sea.

Yet the Philippines could not treat energy policy as separate from territorial conflict. Chinese coercion and assertiveness in disputed waters remained a live concern, and any joint-development idea faced both constitutional and sovereignty complications.

Security ties with other partners also deepened. Japan joined Balikatan exercises with the Philippines and the United States in a historically significant way, while France signed a visiting-forces arrangement with Manila. These moves showed that even if the Philippines considered tactical accommodation with China on energy, it was simultaneously broadening external security support.
